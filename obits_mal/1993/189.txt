
        Alexander Hehmeyer, who served as legal counsel to Time Inc., the United States Government and three generations of the Marshall Field family, died on Feb. 16 at Northwestern Memorial Hospital in Chicago. He was 82 and lived in Chicago.
        He died of a heart attack, said Virginia Butts, public relations counsel to Marshall Field 5th.
        Mr. Hehmeyer began his long association with the Field family in 1946 as a partner in the law firm of Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison, general counsel to Field Enterprises Inc. He represented Marshall Field 3d in the purchase of The Chicago Times in 1947 and Marshall Field 4th in buying The Chicago Daily News in 1959. He became executive vice president of Field Enterprises in 1967, retiring in 1975.
        He began his legal career in 1936 at Cravath, Swaine & Moore, a New York law firm, as legal counsel to Time Inc. In 1940, he left the law firm to become administrative assistant to Henry R. Luce for three years. Appointed by Reagan
        He also served as legal consultant on various governmental missions, including a United States Economic Survey Mission to West Berlin in 1952 and a 1954 Anglo-American survey of Gold Coast, which later became Ghana.
        A native New Yorker, he graduated from Yale University in 1932, attended Harvard Law School and received his law degree from Columbia University in 1935.
        He is author of "Time for Change" (Farrar & Rinehart, 1943), a proposal for a second Constitutional Convention to streamline the United States Government.
        His first wife, Florence Isabelle Millar, died in 1967.
        He is survived by his wife, the former Sheila McArthur; a son, Alexander Millar Hehmeyer, in the San Francisco Bay Area; a daughter, Christine Rosso, of Chicago; a stepdaughter, Gillian Steel, of London, and seven grandchildren.

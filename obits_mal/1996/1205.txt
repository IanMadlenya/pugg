
        Joseph Mark LaRocca, who spent more than half a century working with disabled people around the world, died on Sept. 27 at his home in Silver Spring, Md. He was 87.
        He was an associate of some early leaders in rehabilitation medicine and vocational rehabilitation, including Dr. Howard A. Rusk and Mary E. Switzer. At his death, Mr. LaRocca was a senior consultant for the World Rehabilitation Fund of New York, which trains doctors and provides services in physical medicine and rehabilitation.
        He was was born in Cleveland, and began public service in 1934 after graduating from Cornell University and completing his training at the Graduate School of Applied Social Sciences of Western Reserve University. Early in his career he headed New Deal programs for sharecroppers in Georgia and Florida.
        Mr. LaRocca retired in 1967 from the Vocational Rehabilitation Administration of the Department of Health, Education and Welfare, where he was chief of the division of international rehabilitation services. He helped write Social Security regulations and the Vocational Rehabilitation Act of 1954.
        After retiring, he was a consultant in drafting the Disabilities Act of 1990. He advised the State Department and agencies that included the Urban Institute on setting up medical and vocational rehabilitation programs.
        His international work included efforts to bring together Greek and Turkish doctors on Cyprus, and working in a similar manner with doctors from opposing factions in Lebanon. He also trained doctors in Laos and Vietnam to treat people wounded by land mines left in place after the Vietnam War.
        Mr. LaRocca is survived by his wife of 58 years, Margaret Hunter LaRocca; a daughter, Margaret L. Bishow of Takoma Park, Md.; a son, Robert J., of Philadelphia; a brother, Charles, of Cleveland; a sister, Mildred Wright of Portland, Ore., and two grandchildren.

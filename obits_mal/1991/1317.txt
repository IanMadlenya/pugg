
        Dr. Henry Ross, a New York surgeon who was chief of the public health section of Gen. Dwight D. Eisenhower's command in Europe in World War II, died yesterday at his home in Manhattan. He was 89 years old.
        Dr. Ross died of complications from bowel cancer, a spokesman for the family said.
        A graduate of the West Point in 1926, he served with the Army at Fort Niagara, N.Y., from 1926 to 1927. He graduated from the University of Rochester in 1928 and from Harvard Medical School in 1932.
        In 1939 Dr. Ross opened a private practice in Manhattan and in 1940 met and married Glenda Farrell, the actress. He was affiliated with Doctor's Hospital and was the house physician for various hotels in Manhattan including the Lexington, Essex House and the Hilton. He was also flight surgeon for British Airways from the end of World War II to the early 1980's. He retired in 1985.  Trained as Flight Surgeon
        At the onset of World War II, Dr. Ross enlisted in the Army and was sent to flight surgeon's school in San Antonio. Eventually he was transfered to West Point to serve as resident flight surgeon and head of the hospital at Stewart Field.
        After D Day, Dr. Ross was ordered to Washington as part of Gen. Eisenhower's mission to France. He was later honored by the French Government for quelling a cholora epidemic in Provence.
        Dr. Ross, who was a colonel, was one of the first high-ranking American medical officers to enter the concentration camps. He reported to General Eisenhower on the horrific conditions there and was ordered to organize the French and German medical teams to treat the prisoners.
        Dr. Ross's wife, the actress Glenda Farrell, died in 1971. He is survived by a sister, Florence Cohen of Columbus, Ohio.


        James F. Garrett, executive vice president of the World Rehabilitation Fund, which trains doctors and provides services in physical medicine and rehabilitation, died Tuesday at the Hospice of Northern Virginia in Arlington. He was 75 years old and lived in Falls Church, Va.
        He died of cancer, said Howard A. Rusk Jr., president of the fund.
        Dr. Garrett spent most of his career as a champion for the disabled in Federal positions in Washington, fostering research and training programs in vocational rehabilitation.  Years With Government
        He joined the World Rehabilitation Fund in 1978 and remained executive vice president until his death. He was also a clinical professor at New York University Medical Center and was affiliated with the International Center for the Disabled in Manhattan.
        Dr. Garrett worked for the Department of Health, Education and Welfare in Washington from 1951 to 1978, when he retired as executive director of research and evaluation in the Rehabilitation Services Administration of the Office of Human Development.
        He went to Washington in 1944 with the Veterans Administration, where he helped to develop programs that stressed treating the disabled as a total person.  Awards for Service
        Dr. Garrett received the Albert and Mary Lasker Award for distinguished international service to the handicapped, and the Howard Rusk award for outstanding contributions in the field of rehabilitation.
        He was a graduate of Fordham University and received a Ph.D. from New York University in 1941.
        He is survived by his wife, the former Marie Croghan; two daughters, Patricia of Chapel Hill, N.C., and Ann Caruso of Tulsa, Okla.; a son. James F. Jr. of Long Beach, L.I., and four grandchildren.

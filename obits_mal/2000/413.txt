
        Alfred R. Wentworth, who as a Chase Manhattan executive in 1972 opened the first office of an American bank in the Soviet Union in more than 50 years, died on April 13 in Sarasota, Fla. He was 80.
        Mr. Wentworth joined the international department of the Chase National Bank in 1946. The bank merged with the Bank of Manhattan in 1955 to form Chase Manhattan, and Mr. Wentworth became a senior vice president in 1968.
        He was chosen to open the company's branch in Moscow in 1972. During his tenure from 1972 to 1975, the United States became the Soviet Union's main Western trading partner. In 1974, Chase Manhattan and the State Bank of the Soviet Union agreed to exchange business information, assisting Moscow's ability to trade with the West.
        In 1977, Mr. Wentworth became president and chief executive of Chase Manhattan Canada. He retired in 1980.
        Born in Reno, Nev., Mr. Wentworth graduated from the University of Pennsylvania's Wharton School in 1943, and served in the army during World War II.
        He is survived by his wife, Nancy, of Sarasota; and two sons, Alfred R. Jr., of Jackson, N.J., and Robert, of Gainesville, Fla.

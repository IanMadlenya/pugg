
        John Thompson, a professor, literary critic and poet, died on June 24 at his home in Manhattan. He was 84.
        Mr. Thompson was a professor of English at the State University of New York at Stony Brook from 1965 to 1983, and was the author of an acclaimed text on the history of British poetics, ''The Founding of English Meter,'' which was published in 1961.
        He contributed reviews and articles to many publications, including Harper's Magazine, The Partisan Review, The Kenyon Review, Commentary and The New York Review of Books.
        He also wrote a volume of poetry, ''The Talking Girl and Other Poems,'' which was published in 1968 and was nominated for a National Book Award that year.
        Mr. Thompson graduated magna cum laude in 1940 from Kenyon College, where he was a friend of Robert Lowell. He served in the United States Army and Air Force during World War II, and studied for his Ph.D. at Columbia University from 1946 to 1956. ''The Founding of English Meter'' was his dissertation.
        From 1956 to 1965 he worked as the executive director of the Farfield Foundation, which sponsored plays, magazines and other cultural activities in Africa and Europe.
        Mr. Thompson's first marriage, to Helen Keeler Burke, ended in divorce. He is survived by his wife, Susan Otis Thompson; two children from his first marriage, Louise Thompson, of Manhattan, and Keeler Thompson, of Yorba Linda, Calif.; a sister, Margaret Blanton, of Thomasville, Ga.; and seven grandchildren. A son, Peter, died in 1972.

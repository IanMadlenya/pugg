        Charles S. Lyon, professor emeritus of New York University Law School and a former United States assistant attorney general, died Friday at the Village Nursing Home in Manhattan, where he lived. He had suffered complications from a series of strokes. He was 72 years old.
        Mr. Lyon was also deputy chief prosecutor at the Nuremberg trials of Nazi war criminals following World War II.
        He was appointed an assistant attorney general in the Justice Department in 1952. In 1955, he joined the faculty of New York University Law School, where he was chairman of the tax law department. He retired in 1987, and a chair has been endowed in his name.
        Mr. Lyon was a founding partner in the Manhattan law firm that is now Skadden, Arps, Slate, Meagher & Flom, which he left in 1951 to become counsel for the House Ways and Means Committee.
        Born in Atlanta, N.Y., Mr. Lyon graduated from Swarthmore College and Columbia University Law School.
        He is survived by his wife, who writes under the name of Andy Logan for The New Yorker magazine; two sons, Brooks of Canandaigua, N.Y. and Jonathan of New York City; a daughter, Emily of Cambridge, Mass. and one granddaughter.

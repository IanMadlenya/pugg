
        Nelson Buhler, a lawyer who helped to establish the Baruch Foundation on the environment and was a prosecutor in a Japanese war crimes trial, died on Tuesday. He was 77 years old and lived in Fort Lauderdale, Fla.
        His family said he died of a heart attack at North Beach Hospital in Fort Lauderdale.
        Mr. Buhler was for many years a senior partner in the law firm of Buhler, King & Buhler in Manhattan. His specialties were estates, trusts and foundations. He was the lead lawyer for Bernard Baruch, the Wall Street financier, in creating a foundation for nature conservation and preservation.
        During World War II, Mr. Buhler served for four years in General Douglas MacArthur's Army legal division on the judge advocate general's staff.
        In 1946 he won the conviction of two Japanese for their part in decapitating an American flier when he was dragged from the wreckage of his B-29 after a bombing raid over Tokyo.
        Mr. Buhler was born in Columbus, Ga., and grew up there and in Manhattan. He graduated from Columbia College in 1936 and received a law degree from New York University in 1940. Several years ago he moved to Florida, where he continued practicing law.
        Surviving are his wife of 44 years, the former Sydney Sonn; a son, Nelson Jay Buhler of Fort Lauderdale; a daughter, Millary Morton of Stamford, Conn.; two sisters, Carol Mann of Manhattan and Doris Kaufman of Sarasota, Fla., and two grandchildren.

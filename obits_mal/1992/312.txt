
        Phillips Perera, a business executive and an expert in finance for agriculture and food companies in developing countries, died March 2 at his home in Manhattan. He was 58 years old.
        He died of cancer, his family said.
        Mr. Perera founded and headed Interfin, a financial-service company for international food businesses.
        Born in Boston, he grew up there and in Washington. After graduating from Harvard, he served in the Air Force, rising to captain. In 1961 he graduated from Centre d'Etudes Industrielles, a business school in Switzerland.
        For the next several years he worked for American Export Lines, First National Bank of Boston and the Arthur D. Little consulting firm.
        Drawing on his international travels and research, he wrote "Development Finance, Institutions, Problems and Prospects" (Praeger, 1968), a book used in business schools.
        From 1968 to 1970 he was the acting deputy director of the Office of Foreign Direct Investments of the United States Department of Commerce.
        Later he worked for the American Express Company, serving as vice president of corporate development and a board member of its Investment Management Company before starting his own firm in 1976.
        He was a trustee of the Middlesex School and a vestryman at St. Mark's Church in Mount Kisco, N.Y.
        Surviving are his wife, Frederica Plimpton Drinkwater; two sons, Phillips Jr. and Christopher, and a daughter, Frederica Sophia Perera, all of Manhattan; his father, Guido R. of Boston, and three brothers, Guido Jr. of Lincoln, Mass., Lawrence T. of Boston and Ronald C. of Northampton, Mass.
        Correction:  March 13, 1992, Friday
        An obituary on Monday about Phillips Perera, a business executive, omitted a survivor. He is a son, Alexander L. Perera of Manhattan.

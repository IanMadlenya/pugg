
        Seymour D. Lewis, a lawyer who specialized in antitrust litigation and was also active in Jewish community affairs, died on Sunday at Beth Israel Hospital in Manhattan. He was 79 years old and lived in Larchmont, N.Y.
        He died of pneumonia, said his brother-in-law Max Freund.
        Mr. Lewis was a former senior partner in the Manhattan-based law firm of Rosenman Colin Freund Lewis & Cohen, now Rosenman & Colin. He was of counsel to the firm at the time of his death.
        He was a former chairman of the lawyers' division of the United Jewish Appeal and of the Israel Bonds Greater New York lawyers division. The State of Israel Bonds Committee honored Mr. Lewis in 1982 for his "outstanding efforts on behalf of Israel's economic development."
        Born in Manhattan, Mr. Lewis graduated from Dartmouth in 1934 and from Yale Law School in 1938.
        Mr. Lewis was a special assistant to the United States Attorney General from 1939 to 1947 and was chief of the New York Office of the Department of Justice's Antitrust Division in 1947.
        He drafted consumer-protection regulations that were enacted into law in 1970 by the New York State Legislature, and remain the basic consumer-protection statute in New York.
        He is survived by his wife of 53 years, the former Bernice Libman; a son, Lawrence, of Cambridge, Mass.; a daughter, Ann Lewis Seltzer of Manhattan; two brothers, Merwin and George, both of Manhattan; two sisters, Serena Freund of Manhattan and Frances Butterweich, who lives near Boca Raton, Fla.; and three grandchildren.

        Andrew Jackson Bennett, an economist who advised the United States military government in Germany after World War II, died of pneumonia on Tuesday at North Carolina Memorial Hospital in Chapel Hill, N.C. He was 86 years old and lived in Chapel Hill.
        As an adviser in Germany, Mr. Bennett helped bring about the 1948 currency changes that aided in the country's recovery after the war.
        Mr. Bennett, who used the name of Jack Bennett, was born in Vienna, Ga., and was a commercial banker in Macon before joining the United States Treasury Department in 1930 as a receiver of bankrupt banks. In 1945 he became finance adviser to Gen. Lucius D. Clay, the Military Governor of the American Zone in Germany.
        From 1949 to 1952 he served in Ethiopia as governor there for the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund. After a period as a financial consultant in Washington he became President Eisenhower's economic representative in South Korea. He later served as regional director of the United States foreign aid program for the Near East and South Asia and as director of the Agency for International Development mission in Ceylon.
        Mr. Bennett is survived by his wife, the former Myrtle McInnis; a son, Jack F. Bennett of Greenwich, Conn., four grandchildren and three great-grandchildren.

        Clarence Lawson, a sculptor and painter, died of pneumonia at Lakeside Veterans Medical Center in Chicago on Monday. He was 78 years old and lived in Sag Harbor, L.I., and Chicago.
        In 1938 he became the first black artist to receive the James Nelson Raymond Study and Travel Fellowship of the Art Institute of Chicago. His work is now in the Art Institute's collection. In 1951, the Metropolitan Museum of Art invited Mr. Lawson to participate in a national sculpture exhibition.
        During World War II, Mr. Lawson served in the Navy, teaching arts and crafts to wounded sailors returned to the Great Lakes Naval Station.
        Since 1973, Mr. Lawson lived in Sag Harbor where he designed and built the house in which he painted. He returned to Chicago for health reasons.
        Mr. Lawson is survived by his wife, Della Emery Lawson.


        Nelson Butters, a professor of psychiatry at the University of California at  San Diego and an expert on memory and memory disorders, died on Nov. 18 at his home in San Diego. He was 58.
        Dr. Butters died of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, or Lou Gehrig's disease, his colleagues said.
        Dr. Butters, who was also chief of the psychology service of the Department  of Veterans Affairs Medical Center in San Diego, published more than 240 scientific papers and book chapters.
        He was best known for his work on memory systems and disorders. In addition to his studies of amnesia and dementia, he researched the recovery of function after brain damage and the effects of chronic alcohol use on cognition and memory.
        He was editor in chief of the journal Neuropsychology and had been president  of the International Neuropsychological Society and the division of clinical neuropsychology of the American Psychological Association.
        Dr. Butters grew up in the Boston area and received a bachelor's degree from  Boston University, where he was valedictorian. He began his research on learning and memory at Clark University in Worcester, Mass., where he received a doctorate.
        Surviving are his wife, Arlene; his mother, Rhoda Butters, of Brookline, Mass.; a son, Paul, of Carlsbad, Calif.; two daughters, Lisa Butters of Brookline and Meryl Butters of Pittsburgh, and three grandchildren.

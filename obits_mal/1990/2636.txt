
        Fritz Eichenberg, a printmaker and illustrator of 19th-century classics, Russian novels and children's books, died on Friday at his home in Peace Dale, R.I. He was 89 years old.
        He died of complications from Parkinson's disease, said his wife, Antonie.
        Books in which Mr. Eichenberg's illustrations appear include "Tales of Edgar Allan Poe"; "The Idiot" and "The Possessed," by Dostoyevsky; "Fathers and Sons," by Turgenev; "The Long Loneliness: The Autobiography of Dorothy Day," and "A Hymnal for Friends," the first formal hymnal produced by the Quakers.  Life in Academia
        He also devoted much time to teaching his craft, and a frequent lecture topic was "The Art of Teaching Art." He was also the chairman of the department of graphic arts and illustration at Pratt Institute, and he founded the Pratt Center for Contemporary Printmaking. He later became the chairman of the art department at the University of Rhode Island. He retired in 1974.
        He produced a visual anthology, "The Art of the Print," with 749 illustrations, and he was author of "The Wood and the Engraver." He also contributed freelance work to The New York Times.  Germany and the U.S.
        He had already begun his career before he immigrated to the United States from Cologne, Germany. In the 1930's, he worked for the Works Progress Administration's Federal arts project.
        He became a leading illustrator of Russian classics, and with a group of fellow Quakers he met with Nikita S. Khrushchev in 1960 in New York. The meeting resulted in an exhibition of American graphic art in the Soviet Union.
        In addition to his wife, he is survived by a daughter, Suzanne Jensen of Cambridge, Mass., and a son, Timothy, of San Francisco.

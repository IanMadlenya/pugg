        Dr. Lewis Leary, a specialist in American literature who was the William Rand Kenan Jr. Professor of English at the University of North Carolina from 1968 to 1976, died of pneumonia on Tuesday at his home in Chapel Hill, N.C. He was 84 years old.
        Dr. Leary taught at Columbia University from 1952 to 1968 and was chairman of its English department from 1962 to 1968.
        He wrote or edited more than 40 books, many on colonial and 19th-century literature. His subjects included Ralph Waldo Emerson, Herman Melville, Henry David Thoreau, Mark Twain, Parson Weems and John Greenleaf Whittier. Dr. Leary wrote ''That Rascal Freneau'' in 1941, an acclaimed biography of Philip Freneau, the 18th-century poet and journalist.
        Dr. Leary was born in Blauvelt, N.Y. He received a bachelor's degree from the University of Vermont and taught at the American University in Beirut for three years before beginning graduate study at Columbia, where he received a master's and a doctorate.
        In 1935 he joined the University of Miami faculty and in 1941 he moved to Duke University. In World War II he worked for the Office of Strategic Services in the Middle East, returning to Duke at the war's end.
        The Modern Language Association of America gave Dr. Leary the Jay B. Hubbell Medal in 1975 for achievement in the study of American literature. He was a past president of the South Atlantic Modern Language Associaton.
        Surviving are his wife, the former Mary Hudson; two daughters, Carolyn Bartholet of Lavallette, N.J., and Martha Allen of Corto Madera, Calif.; a sister, Mrs. Horace Peck of Manhattan, and three granddaughters.

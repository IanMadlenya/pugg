        Francis X. Fay, an F.B.I. agent active in the investigation of the kidnapping of the son of Charles A. Lindbergh in the early 30's, died of pneumonia on Saturday at the Little Company of Mary Pavilion in Torrance, Calif. He was 92 years old and lived in Norwalk, Conn.
        Mr. Fay joined the Federal Bureau of Investigation in 1922 after serving in World War I and after graduating with a degree in electrical engineering from Pratt Institute.
        He worked in F.B.I. offices in Chicago, Washington and Pittsburgh and was named agent in charge in New York in 1934. He used his engineering training to develop techniques of two-way radio communication, infrared photography and blood and dye analysis. He resigned in 1935 to become head of security for R. H. Macy & Company at Herald Square.
        In World War II he was chief of Army counterintelligence and helped capture German spies who landed from a U-boat at Amagansett, L.I. He returned to Macy's and retired in 1965. At Macy's, he instituted patrols by Doberman pinschers.
        Mr. Fay founded the Society of Former Agents.
        Surviving are his wife, Margaret; 4 sons, Francis Jr., of Norwalk, Thomas, of Manhattan Beach, Calif., T. Joseph, of East Hartford, Conn., and Gerard, of Ellicott City, Md.; 2 brothers, Vincent, of East Orange, N.J., and Charles, of Greenwich, Conn., and 11 grandchildren.

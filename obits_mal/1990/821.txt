        Ernst Freese, a scientist who was an expert on chemical influences on reproduction and cancer, died of a cerebral hemorrhage on Friday at the Frederick (Md.) Memorial Hospital. He was 64 years old and lived in Bethesda, Md.
        Dr. Freese was chief of the laboratory of molecular biology and director of the basic neurosciences program at the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke, a unit of the National Institutes of Health in Bethesda.
        His research included work on cancer-causing properties of chemicals and on the causes of Parkinson's and Alzheimer's diseases.
        Dr. Freese was born in Germany and was drafted into the German Army at 17 in World War II. He served in Yugoslavia. He came to the United States in 1954 after receiving a Ph.D. from the University of Gottingen.
        He taught genetics at the University of Wisconsin before joining the National Institutes of Health in 1962.
        He is survived by his wife, Dr. Katherine L. Bick; a daughter, Dr. Katherine Freese of Cambridge, Mass.; a son, Dr. Andrew of Boston; a brother, Otto of Celle, West Germany, and three grandchildren.

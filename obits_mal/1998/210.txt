
        Benjamin Aranda 3d, a judge of the Los Angeles Municipal Court and a founder of organizations that worked toward increasing employment opportunities for minorities in the judicial system, died on Jan. 28. He was 58 and lived in Torrance, Calif.
        Judge Aranda, who suffered from hypertension and diabetes, died after going into insulin shock aboard a plane awaiting takeoff at Los Angeles International Airport, a spokesman for the Los Angeles court said.
        Judge Aranda was to have flown to Nashville for the midyear meeting of the American Bar Association to receive its 1998 Spirit of Excellence Award. He was a founding member of the association's Commission on Opportunities for Minorities in the Profession. He was also the founding president of the Hispanic National Bar Association, formed in 1972.
        Judge Aranda, a native of Brawley, Calif., graduated in 1962 with a political science degree from Loyola Marymount University in Los Angeles, where he received a law degree in 1969. He was practicing law when Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. named him to the bench in 1979.
        Judge Aranda and his wife of 33 years, Emma Salazar Aranda, had seven children of their own and adopted four others.
        Besides his wife, he is survived by his mother, Concepcion Calvin of Riverside, Calif.; six daughters, Rebecca Fritz of Santa Barbara, Calif., MariCris, Ruth, Andrea and Tania Aranda, all of Torrance, and Danielle Aranda of Rochester; five sons, Benjamin 4th, Carlos, David, Frederick and Eric, all of Torrance; five sisters, Louise Bauer, Sylvia Grisham and Irene Anderson of Carlsbad, Calif., Kelly Calvin of Riverside, and Tracy Watts of Las Vegas, Nev., and three grandchildren.

        Raymond D. Mindlin, professor emeritus of civil engineering at Columbia University, died after a long illness Sunday at Mary Hitchcock Hospital in Hanover, N.H. He was 81 years old and lived in Grantham, N.H.
        Dr. Mindlin was a member of the university's School of Engineering from 1932 until he retired in 1975. His principal interest, the mathematical theory of elasticity, grew to encompass electrical, thermal, optical and acoustical phenomena.
        In 1979 he received the National Medal of Science, the Government's highest honor for scientists and engineers, for applied mechanics and mathematics. He was a member of the National Academy of Engineering and the National Academy of Sciences.
        In 1946, he was awarded the Medal for Merit by President Harry S. Truman for his work in developing the radio proximity fuse, a detonator for weapons used in offensive warfare.
        Dr. Mindlin was born in Manhattan and was a graduate of Columbia with four degrees.
        He is survived by two brothers, Eugene S., of Scarsdale, N.Y., and Dr. Rowland L., of Longboat Key, Fla., and a sister, Rosalind Elbaum of Manhattan and San Francisco.

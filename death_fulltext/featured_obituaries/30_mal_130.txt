        Brig. Gen. Ensley M. Llewellyn, a founder of the World War II military newspaper Stars and Stripes and a member of Gen. Dwight D. Eisenhower's staff in the war, died Wednesday of heart disease in a doctor's office in Tacoma, Wash., where he was having a checkup. He was 83 years old and lived in the Tacoma suburb of Spanaway.
        General Llewellyn retired in 1951 after serving 30 years in the Army and returned to Tacoma, where he became owner of the Llewellyn Advertising Agency and managed state and local political campaigns.
        He also served as the Washington State adjutant general in charge of the National Guard, as the state's civil defense director and as a representative to the 1980 White House Conference on Aging.
        He is survived by his wife, the former Vera Hoveland Howard; two sons, Paul of Tacoma and Robert of Seattle; three grandchildren, and two great-grandchildren.

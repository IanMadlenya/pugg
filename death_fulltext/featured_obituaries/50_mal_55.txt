
        George Erwin Patton, a prominent landscape architect, died on Wednesday at his home in Philadelphia. He was 70 years old.
        Mr. Patton died of cancer, said his nephew, Phil Patton of Montclair, N.J.
        Mr. Patton, who worked with the architects Louis Kahn, Robert Venturi and Romaldo Giurgola, designed the Locust Walk for the Quadrangle of the University of Pennsylvania campus and took part in restoration projects for Independence and Rittenhouse Squares in Philadelphia.
        Other projects included site planning and landscaping for the Kimbell Art Museums in Fort Worth, the Philadelphia Museum of Art and the campuses of Agnes Scott University in Decatur, Ga., and Temple University in Philadelphia.  New Firm Wins Awards
        In 1954 he opened George E. Patton & Associates, after working in the office of Simonds & Simonds in Pittsburgh. The new firm later won a number of awards.
        Mr. Patton also taught architecture in the fine arts department of the University of Pennsylvania, served on the Philadelphia Art Commission and was an adviser to the Federal Department of Housing and Urban Development.
        He was born in Franklin, N.C., and graduated from North Carolina State University with a bachelor's degree in landscape architecture. From 1949 to 1951, he studied in Europe as a Prix de Rome Fellow with the American Academy in Rome and as a Fulbright scholar.
        Mr. Patton was active in the American Society of Landscape Architects, serving as its first vice president from 1967 to 1969 and later as chairman of its publication board. His writings appeared in several publications, including Landscape Architecture, Architectural Record and Garden Design.
        His wife, the former Sylvia Ott Belleville, died last year.
        He is survived by a sister, Helen Patton of Franklin, N.C., and four brothers, Charles R. of Asheville, N.C., Lewis of Vero Beach. Fla., Andrew of Arlington, Tex., and Mack of Murphy, N.C.

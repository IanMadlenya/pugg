
        William DuBois, a playwright, novelist and longtime editor for The New York Times Book Review, died on Sunday at his home in Farmington, Conn. He was 93.
        Mr. DuBois had several plays produced on Broadway, wrote novels and mysteries and worked with the novelist Frank G. Slaughter on 27 of Mr. Slaughter's historical novels. He also wrote reviews and articles for the Book Review.
        Mr. DuBois's Broadway shows included ''Pagan Lady'' (1930) and ''I Loved You Wednesday'' (1932). Another play, ''Haiti,'' was produced by the Federal Theater Project and opened in Harlem in 1938.
        He was the author of several novels, including ''The Island in the Square'' (1947), set in New York City in the 1920's; ''A Season to Beware'' (1956), about the worlds of journalism and publishing, and ''The Falcon's Shadow'' (1958), about the travails of the theater.
        Mr. DuBois was born in St. Augustine, Fla., in 1903. He graduated from Columbia University in 1925 with a degree in journalism and first went to work at The Times in 1926.
        He went on to become an editor for the Book Review and eventually served as assistant editor of the section. He retired in 1973.
        He is survived by his wife, Barbara Castle DuBois; two daughters, Virginia Blackall of Farmington and Caroline Hutton of Westport, Conn., and four grandchildren.

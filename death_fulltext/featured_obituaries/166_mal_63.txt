
        Ethan Allen Hitchcock, a Manhattan lawyer and civic leader who was a former chairman of the Educational Broadcasting Corporation, died on Saturday at his home in Oldwick, N.J. He was 91.
        From 1961 to 1983, Mr. Hitchcock was a partner in the law firm that became Webster & Sheffield. He was of counsel to the firm from 1984 to 1991 and then retired. From 1959 to 1978, he was also chairman of the Olivetti Corporation of America.
        He stepped down in 1981 after more than a dozen years as chairman of Educational Broadcasting, the parent company of Channel 13, the first public television station in the New York metropolitan area. It was formally renamed WNET in 1970, when it merged with National Educational Television. He was a member of the board of governors of the Public Broadcasting Service from 1973 to 1975.
        Mr. Hitchcock was also chairman of the Lenox Hill Neighborhood Association from 1938 to 1940; a trustee of Lenox Hill Hospital from 1950 to 1970; president of Brearley School, a private school for girls on East 83rd Street, from 1954 to 1960; vice chairman of the New York Philharmonic from 1965 to 1974; and a member of the Yale Council and chairman of its committee for medical and nursing schools oversight from 1970 to 1975.
        Mr. Hitchcock, a native of Milton, Mass., was a descendant of the Revolutionary War hero Ethan Allen and a great-nephew of another Ethan Allen Hitchcock, who was secretary of the interior from 1898 to 1907.
        The younger Mr. Hitchcock grew up in St. Louis and received a bachelor's degree in 1931 from Yale and his law degree in 1934 from Harvard. He practiced law in New York City from 1934 to 1941.
        In World War II, he was in the Navy. He became a lieutenant commander and commanded a vessel in the invasion of Okinawa. He resumed practicing law in 1946.
        He married Elizabeth French in 1937, when he was with the law firm of Hughes, Schurman & Dwight, and she died in 1976. He then married Jane Chase Nicholas, who died in 1987.
        His survivors include his wife, the former Mari B. Watts, whom he married in 1988; two daughters, Constance Hitchcock of Paris and Mary Elizabeth Bigham of Evansville, Ind.; three grandchildren; a great-grandson; and a sister, Elizabeth Gaillard of Manhattan.
        Mr. Hitchcock's wife Jane had Alzheimer's disease, and he was vice chairman of the board of the Alzheimer's Disease and Related Disorders Association from 1980 to 1990 and a member of a federal advisory panel on the disease from 1987 to 1990.

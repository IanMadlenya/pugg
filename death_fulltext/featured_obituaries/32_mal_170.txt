        Darlington Hoopes, a longtime leader of the Socialist Party who was its candidate for President in 1952 and 1956, died Monday at the Leader Nursing Center in Sinking Spring, Pa. He was 93 years old and had been a resident of Reading, Pa.
        Mr. Hoopes became a Socialist in 1914, at the age of 18, and held a number of posts in the party beginning in 1932, when he was elected to its national executive committee. He ran for Vice President on the Socialist ticket with Norman Thomas in 1944 and was the party's chairman from 1946 to 1957 and again from 1960 to 1968.
        When Mr. Thomas declined to run for President in 1952 after six unsuccessful attempts, Mr. Hoopes was given the nomination, as he was again in 1956.
        Mr. Hoopes did win public office in Pennsylvania, however, holding a seat in the State House of Representatives from 1930 to 1936 and at one point being voted the ''most able legislator'' by journalists. Of Quaker Heritage
        He was born into a Quaker family in Vale, Md., and was a graduate of the George School in Pennsylvania. His formal education ended in 1913 after a year at the University of Wisconsin's School of Agriculture. He read law at an office in Norristown, Pa., until 1921, when he was admitted to the bar.
        In 1928 Mr. Hoopes was elected Assistant City Solicitor of Reading, and held the post for four years. From 1936 to 1940 he was City Solicitor. Over the years, he ran unsuccessfully for District Attorney, the United States House and for judgeships.
        Mr. Hoopes was active in the consumer cooperative movement and was director of Eastern Cooperatives Inc., from 1947 to 1951. He was vice president of the Pennsylvania Equal Rights Council from 1957 to 1962. In 1961 and 1962 he was president of the Berks County Bar Assocition.
        He is survived by his wife, the former Hazelette Miller; two sons, Darlington Jr. of Wyomissing, Pa., and Rae, of Elberon, N.J.; a daughter, Delite Hawk of Reading; 12 grandchildren, and five great-grandchildren.

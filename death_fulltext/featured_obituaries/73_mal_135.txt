
        Morgan S. Smith, who photographed American black leaders and celebrities and the life and culture of Harlem, died on Feb. 17 at St. Luke's-Roosevelt Hospital Center in Manhattan. He was 83 and lived in Manhattan.
        The cause of death was cancer, his family said.
        Mr. Morgan became a staff photographer at The Amsterdam News in 1937. Two years later, he and his twin, Marvin, opened the M & M Smith Studios in the Apollo Theater building on 125th Street. Their studio became a popular gathering place for entertainers, writers and artists. Photographed Black Life
        Over the years, Mr. Smith and his brother photographed Adam Clayton Powell Jr., Marian Anderson, Langston Hughes, Jackie Robinson, Sugar Ray Robinson, Claude McKay, Billie Holiday, Bill (Bojangles) Robinson, Duke Ellington and others. He also photographed street life and events at places like the Savoy Ballroom, Small's Paradise, Cafe Society, the Apollo and the Lafayette Theater.
        His photographs won awards and were exhibited in galleries and reproduced in books. He donated 2,000 photographs to the New York Public Library's Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture.
        In the 1950's Mr. Smith built a sound studio at the Apollo, taught himself sound recording and worked in early television documentaries and later for ABC. He retired in 1968.
        Mr. Smith was born in Nicholasville, Ky., and grew up on tenant farms. He and his brother moved to Harlem in 1933. Mr. Smith studied art with the sculptor Augusta Savage and worked as a painter on the Works Progress Administration-sponsored mural at the Harlem Hospital Nurses Residence before becoming a photographer.
        Surviving are his wife of more than 40 years, the former Monica Mais; a daughter, Monica Smith, and his brother, Marvin, both of Manhattan.

        Garson Meyer, a chemist who was a founder of the National Council on Aging and an advocate of programs for the elderly, died Sunday at the Jewish Home in Rochester, N.Y.. He was 91 years old and lived in Rochester.
        Mr. Meyer was a chief chemist with Eastman Kodak Company for 40 years, retiring in 1962. He was a founder of the national council in 1950. In 1961 he was was elected to its board of directors and in 1966 was elected to his third term as its president.
        He was appointed a member of the President's Advisory Committee on Older Americans in 1965.
        He was commissioner of Urban Renewal and of the Economic Redevelopment Commission, both in Rochester, and held many positions in the field of the aging. They included serving as chairman of the Citizen Participation Section of the 1961 White House Conference on Aging, and serving as a member of the National Advisory Committee. He was appointed by Governor Rockefeller as a member of the New York State Committee of 100 for the 1961 White House Conference on Aging.
        Mr. Meyer had been president of the Council of Social Agencies of Rochester and Monroe County, and consultant to the Ford Foundation Study of Community Organization for the Elderly at Brandeis University and served on a variety of boards and committees relating to the elderly.
        He was born in Rochester and graduated from Rochester University.
        His wife, Esther, died in 1985. He is survived by two daughters: Marjorie Meyer of Rome, Italy, and Arlene Weisbond of Sarasota, Fla.


        Milton Shalleck, an ambassador to the South Pacific Commission under President Harry S. Truman and a New York Criminal Court judge, died on Monday at his home in Southbury, Conn. He was 89.
        In 1948, he was appointed as one of two ambassadors overseeing the trusteeship islands of Micronesia, Melanesia, Fiji and New Caledonia. The appointment came after he was chief counsel to the Lend-Lease program and special counsel to the Foreign Economic Administration in World War II.
        He was appointed by Gov. Averell Harriman of New York to be the state's deputy superintendent of insurance in 1950. In 1962, Mayor Robert F. Wagner appointed him to the bench. He retired in 1981.
        He was a founder of the Manhattan law firm of Shalleck & Krakauer.
        He was a native of the Bronx and graduated from the University of Pennsylvania in 1927. He received a law degree from Fordham University in 1930.
        Judge Shalleck is survived by his wife, Rosalyn; two sons, Alan Bennett Shalleck of Princeton and Peter Blair Shalleck of Riverdale, and two stepgrandchildren.

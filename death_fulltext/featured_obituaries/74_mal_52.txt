
        Sidney H. Aronson, a sociologist and educator, died on Saturday at a hospital in Pittsburgh, where he was visiting. He was 68 and lived in Larchmont, N.Y.
        His family said his death was a result of a heart attack.
        Dr. Aronson was chairman of the behavioral sciences division at the John Jay College of Criminal Justice from 1970 to 1972, the sociology department at New York University from 1967 to 1970 and the sociology department at Brooklyn College from 1975 to 78. Most of his career was at Brooklyn College, where he was on the faculty for 33 years until retiring last year.
        He wrote or edited many articles, journals and books. Among his studies were those on the popularity and effect of the telephone and of bicycle riding on society. He also analyzed Federal civil service in early American history, welfare programs for dependent children and India's caste system.
        Dr. Aronson, a native of Boston, graduated from Harvard and earned master's degrees in history there and at Tufts and a doctorate in sociology from Columbia. He was a former treasurer of the Society for the Study of Social Problems and chairman of the Committee on Sociological History.
        In World War II, he served in combat in the United States Army in Europe.
        He is survived by his wife of 43 years, the former Selma Bornstein; a son, Mark, of New Haven; a daughter, Nancy, of Pittsburgh, and a sister, Pearl Feldmesser of Lambertville, N.J.

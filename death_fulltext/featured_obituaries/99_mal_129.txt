
        Dr. H. Easton McMahon, a cardiologist who was the first medical director at the United Nations, died April 20 at his home in Southbury, Conn. He was 93.
        The cause was congestive heart failure, said a son, E. Tompers McMahon.
        During World War II, Dr. McMahon was a medical officer in the Panama Canal Zone and chief of medicine at the Marine Base in San Diego. After the war, he was selected for the job at the United Nations, which then had its headquarters in Lake Success, N.Y., and served for about two years.
        In 1947, he returned to private practice in Manhattan. He retired in 1969.
        He was president of the New York Cardiological Society from 1955 to 1958 and helped to found the American College of Cardiology. He was the first historian of the American College of Chest Physicians and was also on the organization's board of regents.
        Among the hospitals Dr. McMahon was associated with were St. Clare's, where he established its first cardiac clinic, Polyclinic Hospital and Doctors Hospital. He was a consulting cardiologist for several organizations, including the New York State Boxing Commission.
        Born in Brooklyn, he graduated from Princeton University in 1925 and received a medical degree from New York University in 1929.
        His wife of 57 years, Jaqueline Tompers McMahon, died five months ago. Besides his son E. Tompers, of San Francisco, he is survived by another son, Jeffrey H., of Cambridge, Mass., and five grandchildren.


        Charles Faulding, a Brooklyn trolley operator who was among the first blacks to rise to leadership in the transit union, died on March 9 at Parkway Hospital in Queens. He was 81 and lived in St. Albans.
        He had a brief illness, according to the Transport Workers Union of America.
        Mr. Faulding retired in 1991 as secretary-treasurer of the T.W.U. after 48 years as a member and officer of Local 100, its flagship local, as well as the 100,000-member national parent organization. He was the local's secretary-treasurer and No. 2 to John E. Lawe, president, during the 11-day transit shutdown of 1980.
        He was also active in causes of One Hundred Black Men and the N.A.A.C.P.
        Born in Virginia, he came to New York at age 11 and became involved in the union movement after getting a job as a trolley motorman in 1942.
        He graduated from Cornell School of Industrial Relations in 1970.
        Mr. Faulding's wife of 55 years, Lorraine, died in 1992. He is survived by a son, George, of Queens, five grandchildren and five great-grandchildren.

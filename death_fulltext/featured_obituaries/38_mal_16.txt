        Frederick Landis, a former judge of the United States Customs Court, now the Court of International Trade, and former Chief Judge of the Indiana Supreme Court, died of intestinal cancer on Thursday at a nursing home in Carmel, Ind. He was 78 years old and lived in Indianapolis and Scarsdale, N.Y.
        Judge Landis was at the Customs Court in Manhattan from 1965 to 1984, when he retired. From 1955 to 1965, he was Chief Judge of the Indiana court. He was an Indiana State Senator from 1952 to 1955 and a State Representative from 1950 to 1952.
        Judge Landis was born in Logansport, Ind., and graduated from Indiana University, where he also recieved a law degree, in 1934. From 1948 to 1951, he was president of the Cass County Bar Association.
        He was a great-nephew of Kennesaw Mountain Landis, the Federal jurist in Illinois who became baseball's first commissioner in 1921 and barred from the game eight Chicago White Sox players charged with bribery in the 1919 World Series. The incident became known as ''the Black Sox scandal.''
        Surviving are his wife, the former Irene Rhodes; four children, Frederick, of Carmel, Diana, of Manhattan, Susan Wiench of Novato, Calif., and Kenesaw Mountain, of Queens; a brother, Lincoln, of Arlington, Va.; a sister, Betsy Cullen Wright of Charlottesville, Va., and two grandchildren.

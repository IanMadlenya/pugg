
        Robert C. Sprague, founder of the Sprague Electric Company, died on Friday at his home in Williamstown, Mass. He was 91 years old.
        He died of congestive heart failure, the funeral home said.
        Mr. Sprague was born in New York City, graduated from the Hotchkiss School in 1918 and attended the United States Naval Academy at Annapolis, the Naval Post-Graduate School and the graduate school at Massachusetts Institute of Techonology.
        While in the Navy, Mr. Sprague invented a tone control for radio. After his naval service he founded Sprague Electric in Quincy, Mass., in 1926, to manufacture the device and other components. He was its president until 1953 and chairman and chief executive until 1971.
        Mr. Sprague was a director of many institutions, including M.I.T., the First National Bank of Boston, the Charles Stark Draper Laboratory, the Associated Industries of Massachusetts, the Massachusetts Science and Technology Foundation, and the Federal Reserve Bank of Boston.
        His wife, Florence Antoinette van Zelm, whom he married in 1921, died in 1987, as did a son, Robert Chapman, Jr. He is survived by a son, John, of Lincoln and Williamstown, Mass., eight grandchildren and eight great-grandchildren.

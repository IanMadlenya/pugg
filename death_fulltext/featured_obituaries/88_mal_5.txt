
        Donald B. Harden, a British archeologist who was an authority on ancient glass, died on April 13. He was 92 and lived in London.
        Dr. Harden, whose doctorate was from the University of Michigan, was acting director of the Museum of London from 1965 to 1970. He was assistant keeper and then keeper in the department of antiquities at the Ashmolean Museum in Oxford, England, from 1929 to 1956, and director of the London Museum from 1956 to 1965.
        Dr. Harden won particular praise for essays on the history of Roman glass that he wrote for the catalogue of an exhibition, entitled "Glass of the Caesars," which was shown in 1987 and 1988 at the Corning Museum of Glass in Corning, New York, and then in Cologne, Germany, and in Rome.  The exhibition was organized by the Corning Museum, the British Museum and the Romisch-Germanisches Museum in Cologne.
        Dr. Harden's interests encompassed glass made from prehistoric times to the Medieval period. His writings included the books "Roman Glass from Karanis" (1936), about a site in Egypt, and "The Phoenicians" (1962).
        He was president of the Council for British Archeology from 1950 to 1954 and of the Royal Archeological Institute from 1966 to 1969. He was also president of the International Association for the History of Glass from 1968 to 1974.
        Dr. Harden was born in Dublin and educated at Cambridge and Oxford universities as well as at Michigan. He married Cecil Harrissin 1934. She died in 1963.
        He is survived by his second wife, the former Dorothy McDonald, whom he married in 1965; and a daughter.

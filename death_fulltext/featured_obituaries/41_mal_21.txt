        Ethan Hoffman, a photographer whose work appeared in magazines around the world, died on Friday at University Hospital in Newark, where he was being treated for injuries suffered May 26 while on assignment for Fortune magazine. He was 40 years old and lived in Manhattan.
        He had fallen through a skylight at the Wilson Avenue School in Newark. Taken to the hospital in a coma, he never regained consciousness.
        For much of the last five years he worked in Japan. His photo essays appeared in Fortune, Life, Esquire and The New York Times Magazine. He also worked for the West German magazine Stern, Paris-Match and The London Sunday Times. His work was exhibited at the Smithsonian in Washington and other leading museums.
        Mr. Hoffman, a graduate of Hofstra with a master's degree from the University of Missouri, was a photographer for The Walla Walla (Wash.) Union-Bulletin and from 1984 to 1987 was president of Archive Pictures, a photo agency. Last year he founded the Picture Project, publishing photography books and arranging exhibitions.
        His first photo book, ''Concrete Mama: Prison Profiles From Walla Walla'' (University of Missouri, 1981) was nominated for a National Book Award. His latest book, ''Butoh: Dance of the Dark Soul'' (Aperture, 1987), is on avant-garde Japanese dance.
        He is survived by his mother, Ann, of Floral Park, L.I.; a brother, Robert, of Washington, and a sister, Caren, of Faribault, Minn.

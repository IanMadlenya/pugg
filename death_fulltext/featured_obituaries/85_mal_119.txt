
        James L. Robertson, a member of the Federal Reserve Board for 21 years and vice chairman from 1966 until his retirement in 1973, died yesterday at his home in Bethesda, Md. He was 86.
        His family said he had suffered from Alzheimer's disease.
        Mr. Robertson was hired by the Federal Bureau of Investigation as a special agent in 1932. He left to join the legal staff of the Comptroller of the Currency the following year, rising to the rank of assistant chief counsel in 1942.
        During World War II, he served as an officer in the Navy and returned to the currency office in 1944 as Deputy Chief Controller.
        President Harry S. Truman appointed him to the Federal Reserve Board in 1952, and President Lyndon B. Johnson appointed him to a second term in 1964. He was named vice chairman in 1966.
        In his time on the board, Mr. Robertson frequently cast dissenting votes at board meetings. His last important project on the board was to press for the establishment of a Federal Banking Commission to take responsibility for bank examinations and supervision, tasks now divided among the Comptroller of the Currency, the Federal Reserve and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation.
        He retired from the board in 1973 and joined the law firm of Bierbauer & Rockefeller.
        Mr. Robertson was born in Broken Bow, Neb. He attended Grinnell College in Iowa and went to Washington in 1927, when he became an aide to Senator George Norris of Nebraska.
        Mr. Robertson is is survived by his wife, Julia, and a son, Alan.

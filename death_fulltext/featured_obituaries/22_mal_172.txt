        Theodore Morrison, a former professor of English and creative writing at Harvard, a poet, and the author of several novels, died Sunday at the Cooley Dickinson Hospital in Northampton, Mass. He was 87 years old and lived in Amherst, Mass.
        Prof. Morrison joined the Harvard faculty in 1931 and retired in 1963. From 1932 to 1955, he also served as director of the annual Breadloaf Writers' Conference at Middlebury College in Vermont. He wrote histories of Breadloaf and of the Chautauqua Assembly, the public-issues discussion organization.
        As a poet, Professor Morrison's work appeared in the Virginia Quarterly Review, The New York Times Book Review and other publications. One volume of his poetry was titled ''The Devious Way.'' He wrote several novels, including ''The Stones of the House,'' ''The Whole Creation'' and ''To Make a World.'' His last novel, ''Leave of Absence,'' was published seven years ago.
        Professor Morrison also served as editor and translator of ''The Portable Chaucer,'' a standard work in its field that appeared in 1949.
        He is survived by his wife, Kathleen, of Amherst, Mass., a daughter, Anne Smyth, also of Amherst, four grandchildren and two great-grandchildren.

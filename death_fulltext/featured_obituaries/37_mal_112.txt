        Leon Stein, editor emeritus of Justice, the newspaper of the International Ladies' Garment Workers Union, and the author of several books about labor, died in his sleep yesterday at his home in Delray Beach, Fla. He was 78 years old and also lived in Cranbury, N.J.
        Born in Baltimore, he began working at 16 as a cloth spreader in a garment shop and joined the I.L.G.W.U. He graduated from City College in 1934. After college, he returned to the garment industry as a cutter and in 1941 became a full-time union organizer. He contributed articles to Justice for several years before joining the staff and becoming editor in 1952. He retired in 1977.
        Mr. Stein's books included ''The Triangle Fire'' (1962, J. B. Lippincott), about the fire at the Triangle Shirtwaist Company that took the lives of 146 people. He compiled and edited a documentary history of the I.L.G.W.U. entitled ''Out of the Sweatshop'' (1977, Quadrangle/ The New York Times Book Company).
        Mr. Stein translated ''The Education of Abraham Cahan,'' the autobiography of the founder and editor of The Jewish Daily Forward (1969, Jewish Publication Society of America), and edited a 44-volume series, ''American Women: Images and Reality'' (1972, Arno Press).
        He was a former president of the International Press Association and was appointed to the National Council on the Humanities by President Jimmy Carter in 1978.
        Surviving are his wife, the former Miriam Spicehandler; a son, Walter Alan Stein of Manalapan, N.J., a daughter, Barbara Ann Ismail of Short Hills, N.J.; a sister, Eleanor Wineapple of Valley Stream, L.I., and three grandchildren.

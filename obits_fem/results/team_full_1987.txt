Marion Hutton, a vocalist with the Glenn Miller Orchestra who for the last 20 years worked to help rehabilitate female alcoholics, died of cancer Saturday at her home in Kirkland, Wash. She was 67 years old.         Miss Hutton sang with the Miller band from 1939 until the band leader joined the United States Army Air Corps in World War II. Songs she was identified with included ''Kalamazoo,'' ''Chatanooga Choo Choo,'' ''Don't Sit Under the Apple Tree,'' 'Moonlight Serenade'' and ''I'll Be Seeing You.''         She acted in movies including ''Love Happy'' with the Marx Brothers, ''In Society'' with the comedy team of Abbott and Costello, and others with Perry Como, Bob Hope and Jack Carson.         In 1965 she sought treatment for alcoholism and addiction to certain prescription drugs. Thereafter she devoted her energies to helping alcoholics and establishing treatment programs in California prisons. In 1981 she founded Residence XII, a treatment center for female alcoholics in Kirkland, and was its executive director.         Miss Hutton is survived by her husband, Victor Schoen; three sons, and her sister, Betty Hutton, the actress and singer.

She was 65 years old.         Roslyn Lindheim, a professor of architecture at the University of California at Berkeley who was known for her work in hospital design, died of cancer Tuesday at her home in Berkeley. She was 65 years old.         Professor Lindheim was born in New York City and graduated from Radcliffe College and the Columbia University School of Architecture. She moved to California in the 1950's and practiced in both California and New York. She joined the Berkeley faculty in 1963.         She was an expert on health and environmental factors in hospital design and had directed a design team to remodel Montefiore Hospital in the Bronx.         She is survived by her husband, Richard; a son, Daniel, of Washington; a daughter, Susan Lindheim, of El Cerrito, Calif.; a stepbrother, Ralph Colp Jr. of New York City, and three grandchildren.

Robina Gallup, the wife of a co-chairman of the Gallup Poll, died Saturday when a car she was driving crashed into a train at a crossing near Halkirk, Scotland. She was 45 years old and lived in Skillman, N.J.         Mrs. Gallup's husband, Alec Gallup, is a co-chairman of the Gallup Organization, which conducts public opinion polls on politicians, trends and current events.         Mrs. Gallup was traveling to her mother's home in Cirencester, Gloucestershire, when she died. Mrs. Gallup, her husband and several other Gallup officials had been in London to celebrate the 50th anniversary of the Gallup affiliate there. Mr. Gallup returned home and she went to Scotland.         Mrs. Gallup attended Kings College and the London School of Economics.         In the early 1960's she joined the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, N.J., as a research assistant. Her father, Peter W. Mould, a pilot in the Royal Air Force, was killed in World War II.         She managed the Gallup farm in Montgomery Township, N.J., and was a former coach of the boys' junior varsity tennis team at the Princeton Day School. J. She is survived by her mother, Phyllis Grainger; her stepfather, Thomas H. Grainger; and three children from a previous marriage, Peter, Brian and Jennifer, all of Princeton, N.J.       
        Emma Jean Hawes, one of the world's leading women in tournament bridge, died of breast cancer Tuesday at her home in Fort Worth after a long battle with the disease. She was 75 years old.         Emma Jean Hawes, one of the world's leading women in tournament bridge, died of breast cancer Tuesday at her home in Fort Worth after a long battle with the disease. She was 75 years old.         In 1980, Mrs. Hawes became the fourth American woman to attain the rank of grand master, the highest rank of the World Bridge Federation. Since then, four other American women have reached that rank.         In a playing career lasting more than 30 years, she won four world titles and 11 national titles. She represented the United States on four other occasions.         In Venice in 1974, Mrs. Hawes was a member of the winning American team at the first world championship for women's teams based on continental zones, winning the Venice Trophy. She helped defend the trophy successfully in 1976 in Monte Carlo and in 1978 in New Orleans.         In 1980, in Valkenburg, the Netherlands, she and her regular teammates - Dorothy Hayden Truscott, Jacqui Mitchell, Gail Greenberg, Mary Jane Farell and Marilyn Johnson - became the first Americans to win the Olympiad team title in either the open or women's divisions.         Mrs. Hawes retired from competition in 1981.         She was born in Benton, Ky., and graduated from Cornell University at the age of 18.         She is survived by her husband, David Hawes, a daughter, Evalie Horner, and a granddaughter.
